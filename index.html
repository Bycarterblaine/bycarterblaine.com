Every dollar spent on UX, $100 is returned

Introduction

I know it sounds insane. "Every dollar invested in UX returns $100." It came from a Forrester article, and I wanted to provide a hands-on take on why UX contributes the most in a business — after years of working with clients, managing Shopify stores exceeding $100M in sales, fixing broken flows, and watching what good UX actually does.

Why it's valuable

I've seen people dump thousands into ads, social, growth operators — and then wonder why nothing works. The problem is almost always the same: the destination. You can have great copy, decent brand, solid traffic. But when a user tries to buy something and the experience breaks, everything upstream collapses.

Someone spent $4K on Meta and Google ads and couldn't convert a single visitor. The site was barely usable — 2010-era visuals, broken mobile, overlapping popups. With strong UX, 2–8% conversion isn't unrealistic.

What I've actually seen work

Most people think UX is about design trends. The real missing link is connection — understanding what your user is trying to do and removing every barrier between them and that goal. Your message needs to be so clear there's zero ambiguity. No guessing, no hunting for the CTA.

Publishing without resistance

How do you push updates without creating friction? Do it minimally. Test before and after. Familiarity matters more than novelty. That's why full redesigns rarely work — smaller rollouts give users time to adjust and give teams time to correct what isn't landing.

Successfully structuring systems

Building

Pull quality candidates by conversation. Be on the same page in communication — harder than it sounds internationally. Set clear expectations and talk about pay without emotion. Get 10 A+ people around each other and watch them flow. Passive people create passive motives that spread.

Automation

Automation isn't about doing more — it's about removing friction from the work that repeats every week. If something requires explanation more than once, it's a candidate for automation. The most effective automation replaces memory, not people. Follow-ups, status changes, and data movement should never rely on someone remembering.

Stabilization

Stabilization is the point where automation actually becomes useful — leads don't get lost, follow-ups happen when they should, and visibility replaces guesswork. Once that foundation is in place, optimization is what makes scale realistic.

How I took a 2.7% converting storefront to 6.8%

Deleting unnecessary clicks

The biggest flaw was how many unnecessary clicks there were to checkout — a raffle giveaway popup, a marketing enrollment banner, a rewards window on the product page. I deleted them all. Got the clicks from land to checkout from 13 down to 7. I relocated email/SMS enrollment to a pre-checked box at checkout: "Yes, enroll me in messages that save me money."

Ultra-clear messaging

The copy read like everything was 90% off — every exclamation point, all caps. Products and upsells gave off the feel that the product was suspicious. I didn't get all the changes I wanted, but the next day after implementing changes, we saw greater sales right away.

Testing and tracking, everything

I built a table to track everything — baseline metrics before, post-test results after, notes on what moved the needle. Conversion rate, add-to-cart, checkout completion, bounce, time on page. That table became the source of truth. No ego, no attachment — just data. That's how the store moved from 2.7% to 6.8%.

Increasing customer success

I changed their order confirmation emails to read more than just a receipt, built out a rewards system, and pitched the same deals with better color coordination and more excitement in the subject heading. People were checking out and then within days ordering something else — just saying thank you in a better way goes a long way.

Designing to convert

Research what's already winning

Before you design anything, remove ego. Open ten competitors. Go through their checkout. Count clicks. Notice friction. Screenshot moments where you feel relief. That relief is conversion. Winning design usually feels boring until you realize how much money it makes.

Reducing friction through systems

Designing to convert is about respect — for attention, for time, for intent. When users feel guided instead of sold to, conversion happens naturally. People don't want to think harder than they have to. The companies that win aren't louder, they're clearer. Design that converts doesn't feel aggressive. It feels inevitable.

Make it an easy sell

Simplify subscriptions to monthly and yearly only. Reduce to the fewest checkout steps possible. Make every action obvious. Track what happens after every change. I had set up "Auto enable" on one client's Shopify checkout — when the customer went to cart, their name was already selected. Such a small switch, but it has an "Oh it's already ready for me" feel. Those moments compound.
